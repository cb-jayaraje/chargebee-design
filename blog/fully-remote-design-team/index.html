Fully remote

We’re a fully remote
Design team!

These are excerpts from a couple of months into the journey.

As Designers, we thrived in the hustle and bustle of our workplace, the countless arguments, the collective design-debate sessions, collaborative sessions with PMs, Developers, and other teams, the doodling, the tea-breaks, the meetings, the hallway conversations, etc. We did work from home once or twice a week, but that was all. Were there some of us that wished Chargebee was all-remote? Of course!

Then March 2020 happened. Due to CoVID-19, we were asked to start working from the safety of our homes overnight, until further notice. This was six months ago.

So how did we change? And what practices did we adopt to help our designers work remotely effectively?

The early days

When we were in a physical office, we had a list of unsaid office rules:

  • Headphones on = deep work time
  • In a meeting room = do not disturb
  • Designs on the wall = open to feedback
  • Drinking tea in the cafeteria = open to conversations
  • Not in the office = not available

These unsaid rules no longer applied when we started working remotely as a team. The team reported some challenges they were facing a couple of months in working remotely:

Work-Life Balance

Not knowing when to shut down the computers, end a workday, and ease into the evening is challenging. Even post-work hours, some felt the need to constantly check their phones to make sure they weren’t missing any updates.

  • ‘We didn’t know when to stop working!’

Self-Discipline

In a physical workspace, it’s easy to disconnect from the goings-on around you simply because it may not concern you all the time. In the comfort of your own room, your own home, everything matters. The casual conversation between your siblings in the kitchen or the telephone conversation your spouse may be having in the other room can be distractions. Compartmentalization becomes really challenging.

  • ‘It’s different at home. We’re definitely interested in what’s going on outside of our rooms.’

No face-to-face conversations

Remote work does not offer the convenience of face-to-face conversations in the real sense of the term. As designers, a lot of our work is collaborative, and communicating via virtual meeting tools like Zoom and Meets affects the quality of conversations.

  • “Socializing with other designers is difficult. In the office, we used to sit as a team and know what each other was doing.”

Collaboration and Information Sharing

We work in squads. Every product module has a set of dedicated folks working for it. A lot of information and knowledge needs to be shared by members in a squad. One of the key challenges we faced was inadequate documentation and decision points getting lost in communication.

  • “In a physical space it was easier to have conversations with people and get information, but it’s a bit of a task when you’re remote, especially when you have set personal deadlines”

Building a Conducive Work Environment

“Setting up a home space and getting your family members to cooperate was challenging initially.”

A lot of us share our homes with family — parents, spouse, children, or if you aren’t staying with family, friends, and colleagues. It can be quite challenging to make sure that your work-from-home set up is conducive to undisturbed, focussed work. Often everyday conversations, background noises can become distractions and affect work.

We have since tried to address some of these challenges. Here’s how:

Separating personal time from work time

A key aspect of remote work is to proactively separate work hours from non-working hours/personal time. Among the various measures we’ve taken is making sure that our Slack status reflects our availability and unavailability, e.g. by using the stop sign emoji against our names if we are on leave or updating our status with our working hours. We also notify our team on away times on the team Slack channel. This has really helped in making sure that we are not disturbed on your off days.

Self-discipline

We’ve had numerous sessions on how work-from-home/remote work can be made more successful. For example, something we encourage folks to do is block their calendars to make sure they accomplish their daily goals. This way the time can be made use of to complete personal targets/deadlines. The free slots are available for meetings with others. This has really helped us make sure we don’t compromise our deliverables as well as make sure we have ample time to ideate and collaborate with others in the team.

Staying connected

When in office, we had multiple touchpoints for interactions — design-standups, lunchbreaks, team outings, etc. When we switched to remote, we tried to replicate some of these meetings online to varying degrees of success, but our intentions and aims were clear. In our 30-member strong design team, with varying disciplines from product to brand design, we wanted to be able to:

  • Understand what people are working on in the team and give feedback.
  • Build platforms to learn from each other.
  • Encourage informal catchups — tea time/lunchtime chats, etc.
  • Have internal validations from the Design team; we figured there could be similar problems others are solving, so encourage that knowledge sharing amongst ourselves.

From this we evolved to the following format for design reviews and meetings:

  • Design All Hands — Once in 3 months. All sub-teams from Design get a chance to showcase their work.
  • Product Design Showcase — Once in 3 weeks during the middle of the design sprint. This is an opportunity for product designers to showcase and get feedback on the designs they are working on. Attendance is mandatory for product designers and optional for others.
  • Brand Design Showcase — Once in 3 weeks. The brand design team showcases projects they have worked on. Attendance is mandatory for brand designers and optional for others.
  • Design Tea Time — We’ve set up a running slot in the calendar at 4 pm every day for 15 minutes. This is an optional meeting for folks to get together and catch up over tea. People usually ping on our Slack channel to check if there are others joining the meeting.
  • Fun Fridays — Every Friday at 4 pm, folks can get together to do a fun activity for 25 minutes e.g. Listen to a music playlist, play a game, etc. This is an optional meeting.
  • Waffles Wednesday — Waffles Wednesdays happen once a month. This is a learning session where anyone from the Design team can present a topic of their choice — design-related or otherwise.
  • Ops Swaps — This is an AMA/announcement session with the DesignOps team that happens monthly. It’s an opportunity for the Design team to ask about any processes or practices that are followed as well as for the Ops team to share knowledge on any recent changes or developments that happen in the wider organization.

Collaboration and Information Sharing

Before Covid-19 came along, the Design team was already thinking about ways in which we could work independently in our own time. We were working in sprints and had workflows in place in our design tools. We had also started documenting and built up our design hub in Notion. When we moved remote, some of these systems matured and evolved:

Documentation

We set up a documentation hub on Notion for various sub-teams within Design. Essentially, every sub-team now has a dedicated page on Notion with links to all the documents related to work and the projects they’re working on. These are accessible to all in Design. We also occasionally share these documents with stakeholders outside Design.

Documentation was something that we had always actively encouraged. The idea was to keep track of the decisions we make, explain our thought processes, and build up a case of the volume of work that goes on behind-the-scenes of a designer’s work that isn’t just the final mockup. In this context, we started writing design project documents.

Project documents essentially carry the contexts of features like, why are we building it, how does it currently work, user information, other requirements, etc. It enables designers to track where they need clarity, raise red flags, and capture decisions. For Design Managers, documentation helps in leaving review comments asynchronously. Designers can then act on the comments in their own time, as well as make sure projects are moving along seamlessly.

Figma

We made a collective decision to move to Figma earlier this year. The idea was to move to a more collaborative platform where writers and design could work simultaneously and make work happen. Figma has definitely made design work and collaborating with developers a lot easier. Today, all we do is share a link with the developers when designs are done, add relevant notes for them, and make sure they constantly have access to updated designs.

OKRs

As an organization, we’re OKRs-driven now more than ever. This has really helped instill a sense of direction and keeps up the momentum. We have quarter-on-quarter check-ins to ensure that we’re direction bound and to clarify any issues we may have encountered along the way.

Communicating effectively

As a team, we’re fans of Slack. All communication within Design happens in Slack. This makes sure that we don’t miss updates or important notifications. Of course, Slack allows you to pause notifications and set yourself as away if you do not wish to be disturbed and lets everyone know about it, which is super convenient.

All decisions made are captured on Notion and of course, when other teams are involved there are other tools that are used. However, links are shared on Slack channels such that they are available for one and all in the group. This has ensured that all team members stay connected and up-to-date with the goings-on within squads.

Building a Conducive Work Environment

Chargebee announced a monthly work-from-home allowance, and a one-time WFH allowance to enable us all to set up work-from-home spaces to suit our tastes and requirements. This was particularly helpful is making sure that we have everything it takes to make our home offices comfortable.

The folks in the IT team went out of their way to make sure we had no issues connecting from home, setting up tunnel access, licensing soon-to-end licenses, and more.

We haven’t mastered remote work yet

Work-from-home is cumulative of a lot of factors. Some of these factors are above and beyond our scope. As a Design team, however, our attempt has been to minimize any inconveniences, setbacks, or issues our team may be facing to ensure that they present their best selves for work.

We have realized over time that it takes of knowing what the other person wants, what their challenges may be, etc to collaborate remotely effectively — sounds like relationship advice? A lot of great collaboration requires just that — a lot of empathy for the other person.

We’ve miles to go

We’ve certainly come a long way from where we began. We are more in control of the way things are planned in terms of sprint deliverables, review meetings, collaborating with various stakeholders in a more organized and planned way. There is more we need to do, like:

  • Figure out the best tools for communication in a remote setup.
  • Find out new and innovative ways to collaborate with teams outside of design.
  • Find out tools to help check the mental health of the team as well as their physical wellbeing.
  • Measure productivity dips and raises, and find out what triggers the highs and lows.
